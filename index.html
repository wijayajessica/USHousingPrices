Rising Housing Prices

An Exploration of Zillow Data

We’re seeing one of the longest periods of economic growth in US history. When the housing market crashed in 2008, it led to one of the worst economic periods since the Great Depression, often called the Great Recession. The economy did not quickly recover, but has rather grown steadily each year since the crash. Over a decade after the Great Recession, unemployment is the lowest it has been in 50 years, wages are rising at a faster rate, and consumers continue to spend more, which fuels the economy. As the economy grew, home prices rose rapidly across the country. Since 2012, the number of eager home buyers increased faster than the number of homes for sale – demand outpaced supply.

Why is it so hard for millennials to find a home, especially in California?

At its most basic level, it’s a story of supply and demand. What does it mean? Many more people want to live in California, but there aren’t enough homes to go around. However, there are a lot of uniquely Californian factors! From the shape of its coastline to Proposition 13 to the tech boom in the bay area, they all have attached a painfully expensive price tag to the California dream.
The median price of a home is now over half a million dollars. That number is about $240,000 nationally. More than 20 percent of Californians pay more than half their income for housing.

For nearly 20 years, Silicon Valley has led the nation in economic growth. But most of its workers have been excluded from reaping the rewards.
9 in 10 workers in the California region make less than they did in 1997 after adjusting for inflation, according to Everet Program report. The study shows a pattern of income inequality on tech’s home turf that’s even worse than the national average. Authors of the report point to an increasing concentration of company profits going toward the salaries of a select few — largely high-skilled tech workers.

This has been a significant socioeconomic issue of growing popularity:
incomes remaining relatively flat (relatively lower when adjusted by inflation) for people in the middle and at the bottom of the income range, while the cost of living is going up.


Moves by Reason and Income



Counts in thousands

As home prices increase, some people are forced to move, as they cannot afford the cost of their dwelling anymore. On the chart to the right, you can view the housing-related reasons why households of different incomes in the United States moved in 2018 and 2019. Through the chart, we can compare how many households moved because they wanted to improve their housing situation and how many people were economically encouraged or forced to downgrade. We can see that lower income households are more likely than higher income households to move for affordability reasons. However, low income brackets still see people move to better housing and high income brackets still see downsizing.
